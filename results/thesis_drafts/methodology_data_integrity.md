### Data Integrity Investigation

#### Within-Split Overlap Discovery and Resolution

During the Stage 1 evaluation phase, a methodological concern was identified regarding the relationship between the Tier 1 Random Forest pre-filter training data and the evaluation batch sources. The RF classifier had been trained on the full development split of the CICIDS2018 NetFlow v3 dataset, comprising 7.04 million flows. Evaluation batches for the seven attack types present in this development split were also sampled from the same CSV file, creating a within-split overlap whereby the RF had previously encountered the same flow distributions during training. This overlap inflated the RF's filtering accuracy for these seven attack types, although the downstream LLM verdicts remained unaffected since the six-agent pipeline makes independent judgments on each flow regardless of the RF's prior exposure.

The overlap was identified through bootstrap sample analysis of the trained RF model. Examination of the root node sample counts across all one hundred decision trees revealed an average of 4.45 million samples per tree, which at the standard bootstrap sampling rate of 63.2 per cent corresponds to a training set of approximately 7.04 million rows, confirming the model was trained on the development split rather than the full 20.1 million row master CSV. This ruled out cross-split data leakage but confirmed the within-split overlap concern for the seven development-resident attack types.

To resolve this, the development split was further partitioned into an 80 per cent training set (5,632,348 flows) and a 20 per cent evaluation holdout (1,408,087 flows) using stratified sampling to preserve attack type proportions. The RF was retrained exclusively on the training partition, and its generalisation was verified on the holdout set, achieving 100 per cent recall at the established threshold of 0.15 with a minimum attack probability of 0.64 for true attacks. The seven affected attack types were subsequently re-evaluated using batches sourced exclusively from the holdout partition, ensuring complete separation between RF training data and evaluation data. The remaining seven attack types, sourced from the validation and test splits that the RF never encountered during training, required no modification and their results remain fully valid throughout.
