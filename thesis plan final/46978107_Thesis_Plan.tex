\chapter{Abstract}

Network intrusion detection systems face a persistent trade-off between speed and interpretability. Lightweight machine learning models achieve high accuracy on benchmark NetFlow datasets but are opaque to analysts, while large language models provide rich context yet are too slow and costly for real-time deployment. This thesis presents AMATAS (Advanced Multi-Agent Threat Analysis System), a two-tier architecture that combines a Random Forest pre-filter with a six-agent large language model pipeline to deliver both accurate detection and transparent, multi-perspective reasoning chains.

The system separates a high-throughput fast path, where the Random Forest classifier filters 95\% of flows as benign, from an escalated path where uncertain flows are analysed by four specialist agents (protocol, statistical, behavioural, temporal), a Devil's Advocate agent that argues for benign interpretation, and an orchestrator that synthesises consensus verdicts. Unlike traditional classifiers that produce opaque probability outputs, every AMATAS verdict includes full reasoning chains traceable to specific flow features and evidence.

Evaluated across all fourteen attack types in the CICIDS2018 NetFlow v3 dataset at realistic class distributions (5\% attack prevalence), AMATAS achieves a mean F1 score of 92.9\% across thirteen detectable attack categories, with a false positive rate of 0\% in thirteen of fourteen experiments. The two-tier architecture reduces analysis cost by 94.6\%, from an estimated \$509.78 to \$27.35 for the complete evaluation corpus of 14,000 flows. A controlled ablation study demonstrates that the multi-agent architecture outperforms the best single-agent configuration by 25.2 F1 percentage points, and that external threat intelligence tools (via MCP) provide no measurable benefit on anonymised datasets. A v3 temporal clustering extension recovers 58\% recall on the otherwise undetectable Infiltration attack category by presenting agents with multi-flow clusters grouped by source IP.

\section{Background and Motivation}
\label{sec:background-motivation}
This section establishes the growing volume of cyber threats and the fundamental limitations of current intrusion detection systems. Traditional signature-based systems such as Snort~\autocite{Roesch1999} and Suricata~\autocite{OISF2010} cannot detect novel attacks, while machine learning classifiers achieve high benchmark accuracy but produce opaque outputs that are difficult for analysts to interpret during incident response~\autocite{Hesford2024}. Large language models offer transparent reasoning but are too slow and costly to serve as primary detectors~\autocite{Houssel2024}. This tension between speed and interpretability motivates the hybrid architecture explored in this thesis.

\section{Research Questions}
\label{sec:research-questions}
Four research questions guide the investigation:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item \textbf{RQ1:} Can a multi-agent LLM architecture achieve detection accuracy comparable to traditional ML classifiers while providing transparent reasoning?
  \item \textbf{RQ2:} How does a two-tier ML pre-filter and LLM pipeline architecture affect the cost--accuracy trade-off?
  \item \textbf{RQ3:} What is the quality of reasoning chains produced by a multi-agent LLM NIDS compared to opaque ML classifier outputs?
  \item \textbf{RQ4:} How does detection performance vary across attack types, and what are the fundamental limitations of flow-level LLM analysis?
\end{itemize}

\section{The AMATAS System}
\label{sec:amatas-system}
This section introduces AMATAS (Advanced Multi-Agent Threat Analysis System), a two-tier architecture combining a Random Forest pre-filter with a six-agent LLM pipeline. The system is described at a high level, establishing the three-phase agent execution model (four parallel specialists, Devil's Advocate review, orchestrator consensus) that is detailed in \chap{ch:architecture}.

\section{Contributions}
\label{sec:contributions}
Five main contributions are presented: (1)~the first per-attack-type LLM-based NIDS evaluation at realistic class distributions with ablation confirming specialist necessity, (2)~a two-tier architecture achieving 94.6\% cost reduction while maintaining detection accuracy, (3)~a Devil's Advocate mechanism for structural false positive control, (4)~a complete explainability framework with traceable multi-agent reasoning chains, and (5)~a controlled comparison of multi-agent versus single-agent MCP-based detection.

\section{Thesis Structure}
\label{sec:thesis-structure}
This section provides a roadmap of the remaining chapters and their coverage, from literature review through architecture, methodology, results, discussion, and conclusion.

\section{Traditional Machine Learning for Intrusion Detection}
\label{sec:traditional-ml-ids}
This section reviews the evolution from signature-based detection to machine learning approaches for network intrusion detection, covering benchmark datasets (KDD CUP 99, NSL-KDD, CICIDS2017/2018, NF-UQ-NIDS) and the persistent challenges of class imbalance~\autocite{Haixiang2017}, inflated accuracy claims on non-representative test sets~\autocite{Leevy2020}, and poor cross-dataset transferability~\autocite{Hesford2024}. Deep learning approaches including CNNs, LSTMs, and autoencoders are surveyed~\autocite{Ferrag2020}.

\section{Large Language Models in Cybersecurity}
\label{sec:llm-cybersecurity}
This section surveys the application of large language models to cybersecurity tasks, including the dual-use risks identified by Gupta et al.~\autocite{Gupta2023} and the comprehensive survey by Feng and Sakurai~\autocite{FengSakurai2025}. Houssel et al.'s zero-shot evaluation finding approximately 50\% detection accuracy~\autocite{Houssel2024}, Guti\'{e}rrez-Galeano et al.'s fine-tuned T5 work~\autocite{GutierrezGaleano2025}, and Li et al.'s IDS-Agent~\autocite{Li2024} are examined to establish the current performance ceiling for LLM-based NIDS.

\section{Multi-Agent LLM Architectures}
\label{sec:multi-agent-architectures}
This section reviews the theoretical foundations and empirical evidence for multi-agent LLM systems, grounded in Minsky's Society of Mind~\autocite{Minsky1986}. Du et al.'s work on improving factuality through multi-agent debate~\autocite{Du2023}, Chan et al.'s ChatEval demonstrating that diverse roles outperform uniform roles~\autocite{Chan2023}, and Yin et al.'s Devil's Advocate prompting for reducing hallucinations~\autocite{Yin2024} provide the direct theoretical basis for the AMATAS architecture.

\section{Tool-Augmented LLMs and the Model Context Protocol}
\label{sec:tool-augmented-llms}
This section covers the progression from Chain-of-Thought prompting~\autocite{Wei2022} through ReAct~\autocite{Yao2023} and Toolformer~\autocite{Schick2023} to the Model Context Protocol (MCP)~\autocite{Anthropic2024}. The MCP comparison experiments in this thesis are motivated by the question of whether standardised external tool access improves detection quality on anonymised benchmark data.

\section{Explainable AI for Network Security}
\label{sec:explainable-ai}
This section reviews post-hoc explanation methods including SHAP~\autocite{Lundberg2017} and LIME~\autocite{Ribeiro2016}, their limitations in adversarial settings~\autocite{Marino2018}, and how LLM reasoning chains offer a fundamentally different approach to explainability by generating explanations as part of the detection process rather than retrofitting them.

\section{Hybrid Architectures and Cost Optimisation}
\label{sec:hybrid-architectures}
This section reviews existing hybrid ML--LLM architectures for network security, including the two-tier SDN/Kubernetes approach by Kalafatidis et al.~\autocite{Kalafatidis2025}, and the NetFlow standardisation work by Sarhan et al.~\autocite{Sarhan2021} that enables cross-dataset benchmarking.

\section{Research Gap}
\label{sec:research-gap}
This section synthesises the literature to identify four research gaps: (1)~no prior work integrates multi-agent debate with NIDS, (2)~no existing system provides structural false positive control via adversarial mechanisms, (3)~no hybrid ML--LLM architecture has been evaluated at realistic class distributions across all attack types, and (4)~the marginal value of external tools on anonymised datasets has not been empirically quantified.

\section{Design Philosophy}
\label{sec:design-philosophy}
This section explains the multi-agent distributed analysis approach grounded in Minsky's Society of Mind~\autocite{Minsky1986}, with empirical support from Du et al.~\autocite{Du2023} on multi-agent debate and Chan et al.~\autocite{Chan2023} on role diversity. The adversarial Devil's Advocate mechanism is motivated by Sunstein's work on group polarisation~\autocite{Sunstein2002} and Yin et al.'s findings on devil's advocate prompting~\autocite{Yin2024}. A two-tier architecture diagram illustrates the complete system.

\section{Tier 1: Random Forest Pre-Filter}
\label{sec:tier1-rf}
This section describes the Random Forest classifier~\autocite{Breiman2001} that serves as the cost-reduction layer, filtering 95\% of traffic as benign before LLM analysis.

\subsection{Feature Set and Training Data}
\label{sec:feature-set-training}
The 12 numeric features selected from CICIDS2018 NetFlow v3 are listed and justified. The 80/20 partitioning strategy within the development split is described to address data integrity concerns.

\subsection{Threshold Selection}
\label{sec:threshold-selection}
The 0.15 probability threshold is explained: it achieves 100\% recall on attacks in the development set while filtering approximately 95\% of benign flows, prioritising zero missed attacks over minimising false escalations.

\section{Tier 2: Specialist Agents}
\label{sec:tier2-specialists}
This section details the four parallel specialist agents: Protocol (port/flag/protocol validation), Statistical (volumetric and timing anomaly detection), Behavioural (attack signature matching and MITRE ATT\&CK mapping), and Temporal (cross-flow IP pattern analysis with co-IP context). Their prompts, JSON output schemas, and model selection rationale (GPT-4o chosen over GPT-4o-mini and Claude Sonnet) are documented.

\section{The Devil's Advocate Mechanism}
\label{sec:devils-advocate}
This section describes the adversarial Devil's Advocate agent, grounded in group polarisation literature~\autocite{Sunstein2002}. The agent reviews all specialist findings and constructs the strongest possible argument for benign interpretation, with a calibrated 30\% weight in the consensus calculation.

\section{Orchestrator and Consensus}
\label{sec:orchestrator-consensus}
This section describes the orchestrator's consensus rules with Devil's Advocate counterweighting, the three-verdict classification (BENIGN, SUSPICIOUS, MALICIOUS), consensus scoring, MITRE technique consolidation, and reasoning synthesis.

\section{Cost Architecture}
\label{sec:cost-architecture}
This section presents the empirical cost breakdown across six agents, highlighting the Temporal Agent's disproportionate cost (approximately 30\% of total) and the blended cost of 0.0014 USD per raw flow after Tier~1 filtering.

\section{Temporal Clustering Module (v3 Extension)}
\label{sec:temporal-clustering}
This section describes the v3 extension that groups related flows by source IP within time windows before LLM analysis, hypothesising that richer temporal context improves recall on attack types (Infiltration, DoS-Hulk) where individual flows appear benign.

\section{Dataset Description}
\label{sec:dataset-description}
This section describes the CICIDS2018 NetFlow v3 dataset~\autocite{Sharafaldin2018}: 20.12 million flows, 14 attack types, 87\% benign, 53 features. \section{Batch Construction}
\label{sec:batch-construction}
This section describes the construction of 1000-flow evaluation batches (50 attack + 950 benign = 5\% attack prevalence) with seed~42 reproducibility and chronological-by-IP ordering, motivated by Sommer and Paxson's base-rate analysis~\autocite{Sommer2010}.

\section{Evaluation Metrics}
\label{sec:evaluation-metrics}
This section defines recall as the primary metric (asymmetric cost of missed attacks), false positive rate as secondary (analyst fatigue), F1 as composite measure, and cost per flow and cost per true positive as economic indicators. A hard limit of \$5.00 per 1000-flow batch is established.

\section{Stage 1 Experimental Protocol}
\label{sec:stage1-protocol}
This section describes the 14 independent experiments (one per attack type in the CICIDS2018 dataset), each running 1000 flows through the Tier~1 filter at threshold 0.15 followed by the six-agent LLM pipeline. Pipeline governance via \texttt{control.json} and live progress reporting via \texttt{live\_status.json} are documented.

\section{MCP Comparison Protocol}
\label{sec:mcp-comparison-protocol}
This section describes three single-agent configurations evaluated for comparison: (A)~zero-shot with no tools and no prompt engineering, (B)~prompt-engineered with detailed attack signatures, and (C)~prompt-engineered with an MCP tool providing MITRE ATT\&CK lookups. The expected null result on anonymised data is motivated.

\section{Data Integrity and Leakage Mitigation}
\label{sec:data-integrity}
This section addresses the potential for data leakage between the Random Forest training set and the evaluation batches, describing the 80/20 re-partitioning solution within the development split and validation on a held-out subset.

\section{Reproducibility Measures}
\label{sec:reproducibility}
This section covers the reproducibility infrastructure: global seed~42, model versioning (pickle serialisation for RF, pinned GPT-4o snapshot \texttt{gpt-4o-2024-08-06}), feature set JSON storage, per-agent cost tracking, and timestamped execution logging.

\section{MCP Comparison Results}
\label{sec:mcp-results}
This section presents results for the three single-agent MCP configurations: zero-shot GPT-4o-mini (F1=18\%), prompt-engineered GPT-4o (F1=62\%), and prompt-engineered with MITRE tool (F1=58\%). The multi-agent AMATAS pipeline (F1=88\% on the same batch) is compared, establishing the 25.2 percentage point advantage.

\section{Agent Ablation Study}
\label{sec:agent-ablation}
This section presents systematic ablation results showing the contribution of each agent: the Temporal Agent's critical importance (44pp recall drop on SSH-Bruteforce when removed), the two-agent configuration's degradation to 0--12\% recall, and the Devil's Advocate's attack-type-dependent effect (40pp recall gain on DDoS-HOIC when removed, versus 0\% FPR maintenance on other types).

\section{Tier 1 Random Forest Validation}
\label{sec:tier1-validation}
This section presents the threshold calibration results on the development holdout set and validation partition performance per attack type, confirming 100\% recall on attacks at the 0.15 threshold. A random filter control experiment demonstrates that trained RF selection (100\% recall at 7\% selection rate) outperforms random selection (0\% recall at 50\% selection rate).

\section{Temporal Clustering (v3) Results}
\label{sec:v3-results}
This section presents the v3 temporal clustering results, showing recovery of 58\% recall on the previously undetectable Infiltration attack category when agents analyse multi-flow clusters grouped by source IP, at the cost of an elevated 19.2\% false positive rate.

\section{Interpreting the Detection Performance}
\label{sec:interpreting-performance}
This section compares AMATAS performance against related work: Houssel et al.'s 50\% recall~\autocite{Houssel2024}, Guti\'{e}rrez-Galeano et al.'s 99.84\% accuracy without reasoning~\autocite{GutierrezGaleano2025}, and Li et al.'s IDS-Agent (F1=0.75)~\autocite{Li2024}. Attack-type variation is analysed: high scores on brute-force and DoS attacks with distinctive signatures, moderate performance on DDoS-HOIC due to feature overlap, and zero recall on Infiltration under per-flow analysis.

\section{The Explainability Advantage}
\label{sec:explainability-advantage}
This section provides a detailed walkthrough of a representative example showing six distinct agent analyses converging on a correct verdict, demonstrating the qualitative advantage over SHAP/LIME post-hoc explanations. The reasoning chains are shown to be generated as part of the detection process rather than retrofitted~\autocite{Wei2022}.

\section{The False Positive Problem Resolved}
\label{sec:fpr-resolved}
This section discusses the 0\% FPR achievement in the context of alert fatigue~\autocite{Alahmadi2020} and analyses the Devil's Advocate mechanism's calibration, including the finding that it is counterproductive on DDoS-HOIC (reducing recall by 40pp) while essential for false positive control on other attack types.

\section{Limitations and Failure Modes}
\label{sec:limitations}
This section analyses the key failure modes and limitations of the system.

\subsection{The Infiltration Failure}
Individual Infiltration flows are statistically indistinguishable from benign traffic at the per-flow level, producing 0\% recall. This motivates the v3 temporal clustering extension.

\subsection{DDoS-HOIC and Feature Overlap}
HOIC's traffic randomisation pushes flow features into the legitimate traffic distribution, achieving only 58\% recall (F1=72\%).

\subsection{The Semantic Gap in Flow-Level Analysis}
The fundamental limitation of analysing individual NetFlow records is discussed in the context of Sommer and Paxson's semantic gap~\autocite{Sommer2010} and the closed-world assumption.

\subsection{MCP Tool Utility on Anonymised Datasets}
The null tool result (0.3pp F1 difference) validates the design choice to rely on multi-agent reasoning rather than external enrichment on anonymised data, consistent with tool-related risks identified by Maloyan et al.~\autocite{Maloyan2025}.

\subsection{Model Selection Constraints and Generalisability}
GPT-4o-mini's systematic bias, Claude Sonnet's rate limits, and the CICIDS2018 laboratory generation limitations~\autocite{Kenyon2020, Ring2019} are discussed.

\section{Security Considerations for MCP-Enhanced Detection Systems}
\label{sec:mcp-security}
This section discusses tool injection risk, permission escalation, and data exfiltration concerns for MCP-enhanced NIDS, referencing the ATTESTMCP framework~\autocite{Maloyan2025}.

\section{Threats to Validity}
\label{sec:threats-to-validity}
Internal threats (single seed, partition bias), construct validity (three-class collapse to binary), and external threats (laboratory dataset, GPT-4o exclusivity, model family homogeneity) are discussed.

\section{Summary of Contributions}
\label{sec:summary-contributions}
This section summarises the five contributions: (1)~the first per-attack-type LLM-based NIDS evaluation at realistic class distributions, (2)~a 94.6\% cost reduction via the two-tier architecture, (3)~a Devil's Advocate mechanism achieving 0\% FPR on 13 of 14 attack types, (4)~a complete explainability framework with traceable multi-agent reasoning, and (5)~a controlled ablation establishing the 25.2pp F1 advantage of multi-agent over single-agent analysis.

\section{Answers to the Research Questions}
\label{sec:rq-answers}
Each research question is answered with reference to the experimental evidence:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item \textbf{RQ1:} Mean F1 of 92.9\% across 13 detectable types, requiring multi-agent specialisation (two-agent configurations degrade to 0--12\% recall).
  \item \textbf{RQ2:} 94.6\% cost reduction (\$27.35 vs \$509.78) with 100\% attack recall maintained at the Tier~1 threshold.
  \item \textbf{RQ3:} Reasoning chains are generated as part of detection, incorporate domain knowledge, and present multi-perspective analysis with explicit counter-arguments.
  \item \textbf{RQ4:} Performance ranges from F1=100\% (distinctive signatures) to F1=0\% (Infiltration), with v3 clustering recovering 58\% recall at the cost of 19.2\% FPR.
\end{itemize}

\section{Future Work}
\label{sec:future}
Future directions include: v3 temporal clustering refinement (DNS triggers, confidence calibration, Infiltration-specific agent design), adaptive agent weighting (confidence-gated Devil's Advocate weight), evaluation across frontier LLMs (Claude Sonnet 4, Llama-3.3-70B, Gemini 1.5 Pro), production MCP deployment with tool response verification, held-out test partition evaluation, and a formal user study on explainability quality.
